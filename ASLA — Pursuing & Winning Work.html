What's Slowing the Market
The Headwinds — 2026
  • 🏛️NIH Funding Freeze. Proposed cuts and continuing resolution uncertainty are stalling early-stage academic and government-backed research expansions. ARE's government tenant base (1.5% of revenue from NIH/GSA) is under direct pressure. More broadly, NIH funding is the upstream fuel for biotech hiring — and biotech hiring drives lab space demand.
  • 📦Tariff Uncertainty. Construction material costs are already elevated; tariffs on imported steel, aluminum, and manufactured components add an additional layer of unpredictability. Developers are delaying ground-breaking decisions until cost models stabilize. This extends the pre-construction phase — which is exactly when landscape architects should be engaged.
  • 💰Elevated Interest Rates. Higher cost of capital makes development math harder. REITs like ARE are focused on reducing non-income-producing assets (from 20% to 11–16% of gross assets by end of 2026) rather than starting new projects aggressively. Selective, high-quality projects continue — generic market development pauses.
  • 🏢Lab Space Glut. 21.4% vacancy in CBRE's top 13 US markets (Q1 2026). Supply delivered 2023–2025 is still being absorbed. Greater Boston's vacancy spike (86.4% occupancy for ARE) is the most acute. Developers absorbing existing inventory before breaking ground on new campuses.
  • 🌐Global Uncertainty. Geopolitical risk, FDA leadership changes, and drug pricing pressure create a cautious operating environment for the pharma companies that drive ultimate demand for life science real estate. "Lingering conservatism" (Bisnow, quoting ARE, Jan 2025) continues to dampen leasing velocity.

Phase 1 of 5
Scout — What Is It and Is It Real?
Before a single hour of pursuit effort is invested, you need to answer one question: is this opportunity real, funded, and winnable — and is it right for this firm at this time?
⏱ Suggested time: 8–10 minutes
🎯
Opening — Set the Frame
Use this to open the section before diving into details

Start by asking the room: "How many of you have spent significant time and money pursuing a project — writing, teaming, preparing — only to find out the funding wasn't confirmed, or the decision was already made?" Let a few hands go up. That's what scouting prevents.

Scouting is not glamorous. It doesn't feel like design work. But it is the discipline that separates firms that win efficiently from firms that exhaust themselves chasing ghosts. The goal of scouting is intelligence — before investment.

There are two ways opportunities surface: through deliberate research — scanning agency websites, bond program trackers, procurement portals, industry publications — and through industry buzz — conversations at events, a contact mentioning something in passing, a LinkedIn post. Both matter. Neither is enough on its own. And whatever you learn — write it down. Opportunities that live only in someone's head get lost.

🔍
Talking Points — The Research Questions
Walk through each question with the room
  • 1
    Is it real — and is it funded?This is the first filter. A project that has been talked about for three years without a funding source is not a project — it is a wish. Ask specifically: is the funding appropriated, bonded, or awarded? Is there a project number? Has a notice been published? If you can't answer yes to at least one of these, proceed with extreme caution and low investment.
  • 2
    What's the timing?When does procurement start? When is the RFQ or RFP expected? This determines how much relationship-building time you have before the competitive window closes. A project that procures in six weeks requires a very different response than one that procures in six months.
  • 3
    Who are the key players and decision makers?Not just the stated project manager — who actually influences which firms get selected? Is there a department director who weighs in? A community stakeholder group? A board that approves the final recommendation? Map the decision tree before you invest in outreach.
  • 4
    Who is the competition?Which firms are likely pursuing this? Do they have existing relationships with the client? Have they done this project type before? Understanding the competitive field helps you assess whether this is a fight worth having — and where your genuine advantages lie.
  • 5
    Is the scope and fee realistic?Is this project sized for a firm like yours? Can you deliver it at a fee that is profitable? A project that is technically winnable but financially marginal is not a strategic win — it is a drain on capacity that prevents you from pursuing better opportunities.
⚖️
The Assess Step — Can You Win It?
This is where firms are most honest with themselves — or fail to be

After you've confirmed the opportunity is real, you have to ask three harder questions. These are the ones most firms skip — and the skipping is where pursuit budgets get wasted.

  • A
    Do you have a credible relationship with a decision maker or likely prime?Not a LinkedIn connection. Not a business card from two years ago. A relationship where, if you called them today, they would take your call and be glad you reached out. If the answer is no, your first question is: do you have time to build one before procurement closes?
  • B
    Is your experience directly related?Not adjacent. Not similar. Directly related — same project type, same client type, comparable scale and complexity. Selection panels are specific. "We've done parks" is not the same as "we've done three neighborhood parks for municipal clients in this funding category."
  • C
    How does this fit your goals?Is winning this project strategic — does it open a new sector, a new geography, a new client relationship? Or is it just available? Chasing available work is how firms end up busy but not growing. The best pursuit decisions are strategic, not reactive.
Discussion Prompts for the Room
What's an opportunity you pursued that, in hindsight, you should have walked away from at the scouting stage? What signal did you miss?
How does your firm currently track and share intelligence about upcoming opportunities? What's working and what falls through the cracks?
What would it look like to have a formal go / no-go decision process in your firm?
💡
Key Takeaway
Scouting is a discipline, not an accident. The firms that win consistently are the ones that invest in intelligence before they invest in pursuit. Write it down. Share it. Decide deliberately.

Phase 2 of 5
Engage — Understand Client Needs & Issues
Engagement is not pitching. It is listening, learning, and building the kind of trust that makes a client think of you first — before the RFP is even written.
⏱ Suggested time: 8–10 minutes
💬
Opening — Reset the Definition of Outreach

Most firms think of "outreach" as a pitch dressed up as a meeting. The client knows it. You know it. And it creates distance instead of trust. Real engagement is intelligence-gathering wrapped in genuine interest. You are not there to sell. You are there to understand.

The firms that win the most work are the ones whose clients call them before the RFP drops — because the relationship is already there. That relationship is built in the Engage phase. It cannot be rushed, and it cannot be faked. But it can be structured.

🗂️
Talking Points — Understanding How They're Organized

Before you can build a relationship, you need to understand the structure you're navigating. Public agencies, institutional clients, and developers all have different decision-making architectures. Your job is to map it.

  • →
    Who is the actual decision maker — not just the stated one?The project manager may run the procurement. But who recommends the final selection? Who has the relationship history that influences the outcome? These are often different people — and both matter.
  • →
    Who manages consultant relationships day-to-day?This is often your most important relationship. The person who works with consultants regularly knows what works, what has frustrated them, and what they actually value. This is the person you want to have coffee with.
  • →
    How does their procurement process work?Does this client use prequalification lists? Do they sole-source for small projects? Do they have an on-call contract you should be on? Understanding procurement mechanics is not administrative — it is strategic.
💡
Talking Points — Understanding Their Needs & Issues
This is the heart of engagement — what keeps them up at night?

The most powerful thing you can do in an engagement meeting is ask good questions and then stop talking. Most consultants fill the silence. The ones who listen are the ones who learn what no one else knows — and that intelligence is what wins proposals.

  • 1
    What is their biggest pain point right now — at the firm or department level?Not the project level — the organizational level. Are they understaffed? Under political pressure? Managing community opposition? Dealing with a difficult council or board? Understanding the context behind the project shapes how you position your services.
  • 2
    What does success look like for them on this project?Not design success — their success. On time, on budget, no surprises, community embraces it, their director is happy. Understanding what success means to the client is the foundation of every winning proposal.
  • 3
    What has frustrated them about consultants in the past?This question is gold. Clients will tell you exactly what not to do — and exactly what they're hoping to find in a consultant relationship. Listen carefully. Then make sure your proposal directly addresses those frustrations.
  • 4
    Assign the outreach lead deliberately.Who from your firm builds this relationship? It should be the person most likely to connect with this client — not necessarily the most senior person. And plan the cadence: how many touchpoints, over what timeline, through what channels.
Discussion Prompts for the Room
Think of a client relationship that feels genuinely strong. What did you do differently with that client in the early stages?
How do you currently document what you learn from client conversations — and who in your firm has access to that intelligence?
What is one thing clients have told you frustrates them about consultants — and how does your firm address that?
💡
Key Takeaway
Engagement is not a meeting — it is a posture. Show up as a resource, not a vendor. Ask more than you tell. And give before you ask — every time.

Procurement Sequence

How Public Procurement Works for LA Firms On publicly administered projects, the prime design consultant is selected first. Landscape architects are almost always in the second wave. Know which scenario you're in before you invest pursuit resources.
Scenario ALong Lead — Pre-RFQ
→RFQ for prime is coming but not yet live
→Build relationships with likely primes now — before they're in pursuit mode
→Position yourself as the obvious LA sub-consultant when they assemble their team
Scenario BPrime RFQ is Live
→Monitor the shortlist — who made it?
→Congratulate shortlisted firms — open the conversation
→Conduct go / no-go for each shortlisted prime
→Pursue the team most likely to win — and where you add the most value
Scenario C — You Are Pursuing the Prime Role The landscape architecture firm is not a subconsultant — you are being asked to lead the project as the prime design consultant. That changes everything about how you structure your team, position your firm, and respond. The 51% small local business requirement is not a compliance checkbox. It is a central element of your pursuit strategy — getting it right can be the difference between making the shortlist and being disqualified.
📋
Step 1
Assess Your Own Eligibility First
  • ?
    Does your firm qualify as an SBE, LBE, MBE, WBE, or DBE under the jurisdiction administering this contract?
  • ?
    Are you a locally based firm — giving you first consideration priority?
  • ?
    What is your firm's gross revenue relative to the size thresholds for small business certification in this jurisdiction?
  • ?
    If certified — is your certification current? Check the expiration date before you submit.
  • ?
    If not certified — is there still time to apply? Some certifications take 60–90 days. Don't assume you can get it quickly.
  • ?
    If your firm does not qualify as a small business, can you still pursue as prime — and what does that mean for how you structure your team?
⚖️
Step 2
Understand Exactly What 51% Means
Not All Requirements Are Structured the Same Read the RFQ/RFP carefully — the details here determine your entire team structure.
  • ?
    Does the 51% apply to total contract value, or only to professional services fees?
  • ?
    Does it apply to the prime contract, subconsultant contracts, or both combined?
  • ?
    Is it a goal with good faith effort documentation — or a hard threshold that disqualifies non-compliant submissions?
  • ?
    Which certifications does the agency accept — local LBE only, or also state SBE, federal MBE/WBE/DBE?
  • ?
    Is there a priority hierarchy — local first, then state, then federal — and how does that affect how subconsultant certifications are counted?
  • ?
    Does the agency encourage joint ventures between certified and non-certified firms — and does a JV change how 51% is calculated?
🧩
Step 3
Build Your Team — Compliance and Capability
These Two Goals Should Reinforce Each Other Find the best subconsultants who also help you meet the compliance threshold. Compliance and capability are not in conflict — they should be designed together.
  • →
    Map your team needs against scope — civil, structural, MEP, community engagement, cost estimating, environmental
  • →
    For each discipline, identify preferred firms — then check their certification status
  • →
    Prioritize locally certified firms in each category — they count toward 51% and demonstrate local market knowledge
  • →
    If your first-choice firm is not certified, ask whether they have a certified sub or JV partner for that scope
  • →
    Document your outreach — good faith effort means showing you actively sought certified firms, not just that you happened to use one
📄
Step 4
Lead Your Submission with Compliance
Selection Panels Check Compliance Before Design Quality Don't give them a reason to set your submission aside before they get to your portfolio.
  • ✓
    State your firm's certification status clearly and prominently — not buried in an appendix
  • ✓
    Provide a clear table: each subconsultant, certification status and jurisdiction, scope, and estimated fee as % of total contract
  • ✓
    Show that combined certified participation meets or exceeds 51% — ideally exceeds it
  • ✓
    If pursuing as a joint venture, explain the JV structure, certified partner's role, and proportionate share clearly
  • ✓
    Include copies of all relevant certifications as an exhibit

Step 5
Win on Merit — Not Just Compliance
Meeting 51% Gets You Past the Threshold Winning the project requires you to be the most compelling choice on merit as well.
  • →

Position — Relationship Building & Setting Strategy
Positioning is the work you do before the RFP drops. By the time you're reading the RFP, it should feel like you already know the answers — because you do.
⏱ Suggested time: 10–12 minutes
📍
Opening — What Positioning Actually Means

There is a saying in business development: "If you're reading the RFP for the first time on the day it drops, you're already behind." Positioning is the work that happens in the weeks and months before procurement — so that when the RFP arrives, your team already knows the project, the client, the competition, and the strategy.

Positioning serves two goals simultaneously: it builds a long-term relationship with the client, and it prepares you for a specific upcoming opportunity. These goals reinforce each other. The deeper your understanding of the client, the stronger your proposal. The stronger your relationship, the more the client wants you to win.

🧩
Talking Points — The Four Positioning Questions
  • 1
    What is the single most compelling reason you should win this?Not a list of reasons. One. If you cannot articulate it in one sentence, you do not have a strategy — you have a brochure. This sentence should be specific to this project, this client, and this moment. It is the spine of your entire proposal.
  • 2
    Who are the actual decision makers — and are you in contact with them?Not the stated ones — the actual ones. Who recommends? Who approves? Who informally influences? If you are not in contact with any of them, the question is: is there still time, and who can get you there?
  • 3
    Where are you vulnerable — and what is your answer?Every firm has vulnerabilities on any given pursuit. Acknowledging them internally is not weakness — it is strategy. Competitors will use your vulnerabilities against you. Know them first, and build your response into the proposal before the question is asked.
  • 4
    Has your proposed lead been introduced to the client?Presenting a stranger as the project lead is one of the most common and damaging mistakes in proposal submissions. The client wants to know who they'll be working with. If that person is unknown to them, your risk profile goes up — and your win probability goes down.
🚦
Procurement Scenarios — Know Which One You're In
Spend extra time here — this is often where landscape architects are least prepared

On publicly administered projects, landscape architects are almost always selected in the second wave — after the prime design consultant. Understanding this changes how you approach positioning entirely.

  • A
    Scenario A — Long lead, no RFQ yet.This is the best position to be in. Build relationships with likely primes now, before they're in pursuit mode. When the RFQ drops, you want to be the first call — not one of ten emails they receive asking to be on their team.
  • B
    Scenario B — Prime RFQ is live.Monitor the shortlist. Congratulate shortlisted firms — this is a legitimate reason to reach out and start the conversation. Conduct your own go / no-go for each shortlisted prime: which team are you most likely to add value to, and which team is most likely to win?
  • C
    Scenario C — You are the prime, with a 51% SBE requirement.This changes everything. Certification status, team structure, compliance documentation — all of this has to be figured out before you write a single word of the proposal. Compliance gets you past the threshold. Merit wins the project. You need both.
Discussion Prompts for the Room
Think of a pursuit where you were well-positioned going in. What did you do in the months before the RFP that made the difference?
How do you currently track which primes are pursuing which projects — and how do you decide which team to approach?
Have you ever been in Scenario C — leading as prime? What was the hardest part of assembling the team?

Key Takeaway
Positioning is not something you do when the RFP drops. It is the work you do in the months before — so that when the RFP arrives, your strategy is already written.

Capture — Solutions, Messaging, Teaming & Pricing
The proposal is your written argument for why you should win. It should feel like it was written specifically for this client, about this project — because it was.
⏱ Suggested time: 12–15 minutes
📝
Opening — The "Me to You" Shift

Pull up any ten proposals from small professional services firms and the majority will read the same way: "Our firm is highly experienced, client-focused, and committed to delivering exceptional results." Every word of that sentence is about the firm. None of it is about the client.

The single most important shift in proposal writing is moving from "Me" to "You." The client is not selecting a firm — they are selecting a solution to their problem. Your job is to demonstrate that you understand their problem better than anyone else, and that your solution is the most compelling answer to it.

Research confirms the cost of getting this wrong: 50% of RFP responses are rated generic or off-target. That means half the field is handing the win to the other half before the evaluation even begins.

💡
Talking Points — Solutions & Messaging
  • 1
    Mirror the RFP's exact terminology — deliberately.If the RFP says "community engagement in underserved communities," use those exact words — not "stakeholder outreach." Proposals are often machine-read before a human sees them. Missing the language means missing the keyword scan — and that can disqualify you before anyone reads your portfolio.
  • 2
    Understand what they are really looking for.The stated evaluation criteria are not always the real criteria. A client that lists "experience" first but asks three questions about community engagement is telling you something. Read the RFP for what it emphasizes, not just what it lists.
  • 3
    Have it peer reviewed before submission.The person who wrote the proposal cannot objectively evaluate it. They see what they meant to write, not what they actually wrote. Two minds are better than one — and a reviewer who wasn't involved in writing it will catch what the writer missed.
  • 4
    Know your brand position and differentiator.What would clients pay more for? What makes your firm the answer to this specific problem? Your differentiator should be visible throughout the proposal — not just in an "About Us" section that no one reads until the end.
💰
Talking Points — Pricing Strategy
This is where most firms leave money on the table — or price themselves out

Pricing is not math. It is strategy. The number you put on the page reflects your understanding of the client, the market, the competition, and the value you deliver. Getting it wrong in either direction — too high or too low — costs you the project or costs you your margin.

  • A
    Know your brand position in this specific context.Your pricing position is not fixed — it changes by sector, client type, project type, and geography. A firm that is the premium option for institutional healthcare work may be mid-market for a municipal parks project. Know where you sit for this pursuit, and price accordingly.
  • B
    Engage the client on budget before you submit.If it is possible to ask the client about budget expectations or scoring weight on fee — ask. You may be wasting your time if your number will be double what they expect. Some clients will tell you. The ones who won't are telling you something too.
  • C
    Know what your competitors are likely to charge.What have similar firms charged for comparable recent work? Are your competitors going to undercut you on price — making fee the key determinant? Or is this a client who has demonstrated they value quality? Both answers change your strategy.
  • D
    Separate Core from Augmented scope clearly.Be explicit about what is included in your fee and what is not. Identify what was requested versus what you are adding as value. This protects your margin, demonstrates professionalism, and prevents the single most common source of client-consultant conflict: scope creep that nobody saw coming.
⚠️ AI Proposals — Say This to the Room
AI is a powerful tool for proposal development — but it is a fast editor, not a proposal writer. The risk is not that AI produces bad writing. The risk is that AI produces generic writing — and generic proposals lose. Feed AI your specific experience, your actual project examples, your genuine understanding of this client's situation. It will help you express what you already know. It cannot generate the substance that wins.

Nurture — Client Retention & Staying Connected
Keeping the clients you've earned is harder than winning new ones — and far more valuable. The next project begins in the conversation at the close of this one.
⏱ Suggested time: 8–10 minutes
🔄
Opening — The Most Overlooked Phase

Most firms treat the end of a project as the end of the relationship. The file is closed, the invoice is sent, and everyone moves on to the next pursuit. This is the most expensive mistake in business development.

A satisfied client who never hears from you again is not a retained client. They are a past client — and past clients are the easiest referral source, the fastest path to repeat work, and the most underutilized asset in most firms' BD programs. Retention is not passive. It is an active discipline.

The research is clear: it costs five to seven times more to win a new client than to retain an existing one. And a retained client who refers you is worth more than any marketing campaign you can run.

📡
Talking Points — Staying Relevant

Staying relevant is not about volume — it is about specificity. A generic industry newsletter feels like spam. A note that says "I saw the bond measure passed and thought of your parks program — congratulations" feels like a relationship.

  • →
    Tier your relationships deliberately.Not all contacts deserve the same investment. Tier 1 — active clients and warmest prospects — get regular, high-touch contact. Tier 2 — past clients and strong relationships — get quarterly coffee or lunch. Tier 3 — extended network — get a check-in or a relevant article a few times a year. Even a brief "thinking of you" note keeps the thread alive.
  • →
    Share intelligence that is relevant to them specifically.Not general industry news. News about their agency, their sector, their funding sources, their competitors. Show them that you are paying attention to their world — not just your own pipeline.
  • →
    Show up where they show up.Attend the events they attend. Participate in the associations they value. Be consistently visible in the spaces your clients occupy. Reputation compounds over time — the firms that are always present are the ones that come to mind first.
📋
Talking Points — Before You Close the File
Three things. Every project. No exceptions.

The close of a project is the highest-leverage moment in the client relationship — and most firms let it pass without acting. Before you close the file, do three things. Every project. No exceptions.

  • 1
    Request a debrief.Ask what went well and what you could do better. This intelligence makes your next proposal stronger — and it signals to the client that you care about continuous improvement, not just the check. Most clients are surprised when consultants ask for feedback. That surprise is an opportunity to differentiate.
  • 2
    Ask for a reference.The easiest time to ask is immediately after a successful delivery — while the experience is fresh and the relationship is warm. Not six months later when you are in pursuit mode and the client has moved on to other things. A strong reference letter is worth more than any marketing material you can produce.
  • 3
    Schedule the next touchpoint before you walk out the door.Book a six-month check-in before you leave the final meeting. Don't rely on memory or good intentions — both fail. The next project begins in this conversation. A client who expects to hear from you in six months is a client who is still thinking about working with you.
🔄
Reciprocity — Give Before You Ask

The most durable client relationships are built on reciprocity — a pattern of giving that is not transactional. Share intelligence. Make introductions. Offer honest perspective. All without an immediate agenda.

When you help a client solve a problem — even a small one — without billing for it, you earn something that no proposal can buy: trust. And trust is what converts a satisfied client into a loyal one. It is what makes them call you before the RFP instead of during the RFP. It is what makes them tell their colleagues about you. It is the foundation of everything in this room.
