Who They Are
Business model and mission
Alexandria pioneered the life science real estate niche in 1994. They are the only S&P 500 REIT focused exclusively on laboratory and life science space, owning, operating, and developing what they call Megacampus™ ecosystems — large, collaborative science districts that house biopharma companies, research institutions, government agencies, and venture capital firms in close proximity.
Their model is built on the thesis that clustering the world's leading life science companies in purpose-built campuses drives tenant success — and therefore drives Alexandria's long-term value.
HQ: 26 N. Euclid Ave, Pasadena, CA 91101 · (626) 578-0777 · are.com

Strategic Priorities
What they are focused on right now
→Reducing non-income-producing assets from 20% (2024) to 11–16% of gross assets by end of 2026 — a major capital discipline initiative
→Delivering $700M in 2026 stabilizations from active development pipeline
→Improving occupancy in Greater Boston (declined from 94.8% to 86.4%) — their largest and highest-revenue market

→Selectively disposing of non-core assets — including all 10 Canada properties designated held for sale in Q4 2025
→Maintaining life science tenant ecosystem quality amid an industry-wide funding contraction

Understanding Their Risks — Your BD AdvantageWhen you understand what keeps Alexandria's leadership up at night, you can position your firm as a risk mitigator, not just a vendor. Their risks fall into four categories: market and occupancy pressure, life science industry dependence, capital and financial exposure, and environmental/physical risk.

High-Priority Risks

High Priority
Greater Boston Occupancy Decline
Occupancy in their largest market dropped from 94.8% (2024) to 86.4% (2025) — a significant decline. With 62 properties and $709M in revenue, this is their most material near-term financial risk. The decline is linked to temporary vacancies in Kendall Square and other Cambridge submarkets. Re-leasing pressure is intense.
High Priority
Life Science Industry Funding Contraction
A core risk: if biotech and pharma companies cannot raise capital (IPOs, venture funding, NIH grants), they cannot grow — and may downsize or default on leases. With 53% of revenue from investment-grade tenants, Alexandria has some protection, but preclinical/clinical biotech tenants (10%+ of revenue) are acutely vulnerable to funding cycles.
High Priority
$6.35B Development Pipeline Execution Risk
Their development and redevelopment pipeline is massive relative to their balance sheet. Cost overruns, delays in permitting, construction labor shortages, or an inability to pre-lease completed space could materially impact cash flows and debt service capacity. Non-income-producing assets are being targeted to decline from 20% to 11–16% of gross assets.
High Priority
2026–2027 Lease Expiration Wave
$71M in annual revenue is expiring in 2026 from key vacancies with expected downtime of 6–24 months. Another $72M expires in 2027. Re-leasing these spaces — particularly the Stanford Life Science District and One Alexandria Square — represents a significant revenue risk if market conditions deteriorate or competition intensifies.
Medium Priority
Seismic and Natural Disaster Exposure
Significant portions of ARE's portfolio are in seismically active regions (SF Bay Area, San Diego, Seattle). Their all-risk property insurance provides a $2.0B per-occurrence limit for the operating portfolio. Earthquake coverage for San Francisco Bay Area is separately managed at replacement value; Seattle coverage is capped at $200M per occurrence. Wildfire risk (Northern California/PG&E) is an additional exposure.
Medium Priority
Ground Lease Exposure
31 properties (approximately 9% of total) are subject to ground leasehold interests, including 19 in SF Bay Area, 8 in Greater Boston, and 2 in NYC. Ground lease default or inability to renew at favorable terms could expose Alexandria to significant asset losses. Ground lease expense was 1.6% of net operating income in 2025.
Medium Priority
Texas Market Underperformance
At 79.9% occupancy (the lowest of any market) and the lowest rent per RSF ($28.02), Texas is a drag on portfolio performance. The Woodlands campus has not achieved the tenant density needed to create the Megacampus ecosystem effect that drives Alexandria's value proposition in other markets.
Medium Priority
Tenant Concentration — Top 10 = 38.4% of Revenue
Bristol-Myers Squibb alone represents 6.1% of revenue. If a major pharma tenant undergoes M&A, restructuring, or strategic shifts (e.g., lab space requirements change due to AI-driven drug discovery), Alexandria could face significant vacancy in specific markets. BMS's lease in San Diego's Campus Point is their largest single commitment.
External Pressure
U.S. Government / NIH Funding Uncertainty
The U.S. Government is Alexandria's 9th largest tenant, representing 1.5% of revenue from NIH and GSA leases. Federal budget pressures, continuing resolutions, or reductions in NIH research funding could reduce demand from government tenants and, more broadly, shrink the biopharma funding ecosystem that drives their entire tenant base's ability to pay rent.
External Pressure
Short Seller and Reputational Risk
In June 2023, a short seller published negative reports about Alexandria's business and financial prospects. The 10-K specifically discloses this as an ongoing risk, noting that AI-generated content could amplify such campaigns. This reflects management sensitivity to narrative and perception — which includes how their campuses look, feel, and perform publicly.

How Risks Translate to Landscape Architecture Opportunities

BD Insight
Occupancy Pressure → Exterior Refresh Budgets
When buildings are vacant and being re-leased, landlords upgrade the property to attract new tenants. Landscape, entry plazas, outdoor amenities, and campus environments become critical leasing tools. Alexandria's Boston occupancy decline creates direct design-refresh opportunity across their Cambridge and suburban campuses.
BD Insight
Pipeline Execution → Ground-Up Design Work
Nearly 2.3M RSF of active development requires civil, landscape, hardscape, stormwater, LEED®, and public realm design. Are you positioned as a subconsultant to the prime architects and engineers working on Alexandria's development projects? These are the relationships to build now — before the RFP drops.
BD Insight
Seismic / Environmental Risk → Risk Mitigation Design
Alexandria's focus on environmental risk creates demand for stormwater management, resilience design, fire-resistant landscaping (wildfire risk in Northern California), and sustainable infrastructure. Positioning as a risk-reduction partner — not just an aesthetic designer — elevates your value proposition.
BD Insight
Tenant Attraction → Amenity Arms Race
Life science campuses compete for the world's best scientists and researchers. Outdoor amenities, biophilic design, walking paths, collaborative outdoor spaces, and LEED® landscape certification directly help Alexandria attract and retain the tenants that drive their revenue. Your work is a competitive weapon for them — frame it that way.
🌿 The Landscape Architecture Opportunity at Alexandria
39.4M RSF of life science campuses across 7 markets · $6.35B active development pipeline · 19.9M RSF future entitled land
Ground-Up Campus Development
Nearly 2.3M RSF actively under construction. Each Megacampus requires landscape architecture, civil coordination, hardscape, stormwater, open space programming, and site amenities. These projects engage 5–10 consultants before a shovel breaks ground.
Tenant Improvement Cycles
$71M in 2026 expirations and $72M in 2027 create re-leasing activity. Properties going through re-leasing typically receive exterior and campus refreshes — especially on Megacampus properties where curb appeal is a direct leasing tool.
LEED® and Sustainability
Alexandria actively pursues LEED® certification across its portfolio. Landscape architects are critical to LEED® credits in stormwater management, heat island reduction, site ecology, and outdoor space quality. This creates a recurring professional services need on every project.
Megacampus Amenity Programming
Alexandria's Megacampus brand is built on the idea that clustering creates innovation. Outdoor spaces, walking paths, community gardens, plazas, and biophilic design are direct expressions of this brand. These are not afterthoughts — they are strategic assets.
Future Pipeline (19.9M RSF)
Alexandria holds entitled land for nearly 20 million additional RSF of future development on existing Megacampus sites. Relationships built today — with Alexandria's development team, their architects, and their civil engineers — are the pipeline for the next decade of work.
California Portfolio Concentration
Greater Boston + SF Bay Area + San Diego = 62% of revenue and 66% of RSF. For a Southern California landscape architecture firm, San Diego (97.2% occupied, active development) and LA/South Bay proximity to Pasadena HQ are natural entry points for direct client relationship development.

How to Engage — Entry Points and Relationship Strategy

🏗️
Subconsultant to Prime Architects
Alexandria's development projects are led by prime architects of record (typically large firms like Gensler, HOK, ZGF, Flad). Position your firm as the preferred landscape architect subconsultant for these primes before they are engaged on an Alexandria project. Start with Cambridge, Mission Bay, and Campus Point.
🤝
Direct Development Team Relationship
Alexandria's internal development team manages their pipeline. Target the VP/SVP of Development in San Diego and SF Bay Area markets. Their Pasadena HQ (26 N. Euclid Ave) is accessible for Southern California firms. Use their annual reports, project announcements, and NAIOP/ULI events as touchpoints.
🔬
Lead with Life Science Fluency
Alexandria does not need a generalist landscape architect. They need a partner who understands the unique requirements of life science campuses — biosafety setbacks, emergency vehicle access, hazmat considerations, LEED® lab-specific credits, and the amenity expectations of PhD-level scientists. Demonstrate this fluency immediately.
📊
Frame as Occupancy Strategy
With Greater Boston at 86.4% occupancy, Alexandria is under pressure to attract and retain tenants. Frame your outdoor amenity and campus design services as direct occupancy support — not landscaping. "Our work on [comparable project] helped the owner achieve 95%+ occupancy within 18 months of delivery" is a winning message.
🌱
Sustainability as Risk Mitigation
Alexandria operates in wildfire risk zones (Northern California), seismic zones (SF, SD, Seattle), and coastal vulnerability areas. Position resilient landscape design — fire-resistant planting, stormwater detention, bio-retention, and native habitat — as risk reduction that protects their asset value and insurance profile.
📍
Start in San Diego
San Diego is 97.2% occupied, has the most active development pipeline (Campus Point expansion, Torrey Pines, Sorrento Mesa), is a short drive from a Southern California firm, and lacks the re-leasing pressure of Boston. It's the most accessible entry point with the highest development activity. Start there.
