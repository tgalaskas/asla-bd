The National Headwinds — Why Most Competitors Are Quiet
  • 1
    NIH Funding Freeze.Proposed cuts and continuing resolution uncertainty stall early-stage academic and government-backed research expansions. NIH funding is the upstream fuel for biotech hiring — and biotech hiring drives lab space demand. ARE's government tenant base (NIH/GSA) is under direct pressure.
  • 2
    Tariff Uncertainty.Construction material costs already elevated; tariffs on imported steel, aluminum, and manufactured components add unpredictability. Developers delaying groundbreaking decisions until cost models stabilize. This extends the pre-construction phase — exactly when landscape architects should be engaged.
  • 3
    Elevated Interest Rates.Higher cost of capital makes development math harder. REITs like ARE are focused on reducing non-income-producing assets (from 20% to 11–16% of gross assets by end of 2026) rather than starting new projects aggressively. Selective, funded projects continue — generic market development pauses.
  • 4
    Lab Space Glut (National).21.4% vacancy nationally. Supply delivered 2023–2025 is still being absorbed. Greater Boston's vacancy spike (86.4% ARE occupancy) is the most acute. San Diego does not share this problem — 97.2% occupancy means developers are still building here.
  • 5
    Global Uncertainty.FDA leadership changes, drug pricing pressure, and geopolitical risk create cautious pharma operating environments. "Lingering conservatism" (Bisnow quoting ARE, Jan 2025) dampens leasing velocity nationally — but San Diego's supply-demand balance insulates it.

LEED & Fitwel Credits — Name Them or Don't Submit

Non-Negotiable for Any ARE ProposalIf your proposal does not name specific LEED v4 Site & Water credits and Fitwel outdoor space requirements, it reads as generic. ARE's sustainability team reads every proposal. They will know immediately whether you understand lab-building LEED or not. This is the single most differentiating technical capability you can demonstrate — and the most common gap in LA firm proposals for life science work.
LEED SS — Stormwater Management
Bioretention planters, permeable paving, on-site water quality treatment. Campus Point is in a region with water quality regulatory requirements. Name the credit, name the strategy, name the measurable outcome. Show you've done this before — with documentation.
LEED SS — Heat Island Reduction
Native shade trees, cool paving strategies (SRI values), green roofs, reflective hardscape. SD climate creates heat island risk — your design mitigates it. Name specific SRI values for the paving materials you're proposing. Don't be vague on this credit.
LEED SS — Open Space + Site Assessment
Outdoor access for occupants — the "restorative walking path" and "event lawn" ARE already named ARE your deliverables for these credits. Show how your design maximizes these credits while delivering ARE's stated amenity vision. The overlap is your narrative.
LEED WE — Water Efficient Landscaping
Native and adapted plant species, efficient irrigation design, potable water reduction targets. San Diego's drought context makes this credit especially visible to the development team. Show your native plant palette and irrigation strategy explicitly.
Fitwel — Outdoor Spaces (Credits 11–14)
Fitwel certifies buildings on occupant health outcomes. Outdoor spaces supporting physical activity and mental restoration are scored credits. ARE named Fitwel in BMS specs — describe specifically how your design delivers Fitwel credits, not just LEED. These are different frameworks; both matter.
I2SL Labs2Zero — Energy Reduction Contribution
ARE tracks building energy performance using this lab-specific scoring system (averaged 74/100 in 2024). Your landscape design contributes through shading strategies that reduce building cooling loads and HVAC demand. Note this explicitly — it demonstrates sector-specific literacy beyond standard LEED.

Fee Strategy — Headwind Market, Premium Client

Do Not Compete on Price Against ARE — They Are Not Price-Sensitive on LEED DeliveryIn a headwind market, the instinct is to sharpen the pencil. Resist it. ARE is not selecting the lowest bidder for a $6.35B portfolio. They are selecting the firm that can reliably deliver LEED Gold, Fitwel, and an outdoor environment that helps BMS recruit PhD-level scientists from around the world. Pricing too low signals you don't understand the scope — or that you'll cut corners when budget pressure hits.
💲
Price to LEED Value
LEED certification failure costs far more than your fee. Frame your fee as LEED delivery insurance: "Our team has delivered LEED Gold/Platinum site credits on [X projects] without a single credit shortfall or certification delay. That reliability is what your fee is paying for — not hours, but outcomes."
📍
Local Presence = Billable Savings
An SF-based LA firm bills travel expenses for every site visit to Campus Point. You don't. Frame it as responsiveness first, cost second: "Our San Diego–accessible team can be on-site within 2 hours of a call from ARE's development team — without travel costs and without scheduling constraints."
🎯
Scope Clarity Protects Margin
Life science campus projects generate enormous change order exposure — phasing changes, TI coordination, utility conflicts, tenant-driven modifications. Define precisely what is and is not included. Document your change order process in the proposal. Don't absorb ARE's development complexity for free.
